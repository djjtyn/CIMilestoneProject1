EQUALIZATION (EQ)

Equalization, also known as EQ, is probably the most used tool in audio production. It allows the user to adjust the volume of individual frequencies within a sound. This allows the sound to be cured of its imperfections. This is done by lowering the volume of unwanted frequencies and increasing the volume of frequencies which enhance the sound.
An example of an unwanted frequency could be the constant hum of an guitar amplfier. To eliminate this unwanted sound the user would identify which frequency the hum has and lower the volume of that frequency to eliminate the sound of it.
An example of a frequency which enhances the sound might be the frequency at which a snare drum sounds best. For this example, the user might increase the volume of this frequency to enphasize the snare drum hits and make them really stand out in a recording.
There are different types of equalizers that can be used. Below are explanations of two of these types-1 band and 7 band.

1-Band EQ

A one band EQ allows the user to adjust the volume of one particular frequency. It is the most basic EQ type. An example of when to use this would be when everything on a recording sounds very good but there is a hum that can be heard from a guitar amplifier.
The coloured ball at the top of the shaded curve in the accompanying picture shows the frequency which is being adjusted. As you can see the shaded area is very broad and this is also raising the volume of the frequencies beside the selected one as well but this can be adjusted to be a more narrow or broader band width by adjusting the frequency parameter of the tool.
There are usually presets available on one band EQ tools which may aleviate hours of trying to find the specific frequency you are trying to eliminate as there may be a preset for amplifier hum, amplifier feedback etc.

7-Band EQ

The seven band EQ tool is a much more advanced tool. It allows the user to adjust seven frequencies simultaneously using just the one tool.
This tool is usually helpful in achieving a particular sound such as raising the treble of an electric guitar and reducing the bass as well or cleaning up the sound of an untuned drum set.
As seen in the accompanying picture, there are five coloured balls each adjusting a particular frequency. The shaded areas in this picture are also very broad, affecting the frequencies beside the selected frequency but this bandwidth can be narrowed or broadened by adjusting the frequency parameter on the chosen frequency.
